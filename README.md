## Briefly summarize the DriverPass project. Who was the client? What type of system did they want you to design?
DriverPass is a web-based learning management system designed to provide students with high-quality driver's education resources. Liam, the owner of DriverPass and the client for this project, tasked our firm with designing a platform that supports online classes, practice tests, and on-the-road training. To ensure that study materials and practice exams remain accurate and aligned with current licensing requirements, the client also wanted DriverPass to automatically receive updates from the DMV. The goal of the system is to provide students with a convenient, centralized platform for preparing for both the written and practical portions of their driver's license exams.

## How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your system design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?
Successful system development is defined by solving problems for people. Through an interview with the client, I was able to identify the goals, challenges, and expectations that the system needed to address. Rather than viewing the client’s requests as a simple list of features, I interpreted each requirement by considering the underlying problem it was intended to solve. This allowed me to translate the client's needs into specific system requirements, user stories, diagrams, and design elements that guided the development process.

Considering the user’s needs is essential because the success of a system is ultimately determined by the people who use it. A technically impressive system can still fail if it is difficult to navigate, lacks important functionality, or does not address the user’s actual problems. By focusing on user needs throughout the design process, developers can create systems that are intuitive, efficient, and valuable to stakeholders. Additionally, understanding user needs helps prioritize features, reduce unnecessary complexity, and ensure that development resources are spent on functionality that provides real benefits. User-centered design increases the likelihood that the final product will meet expectations and achieve its intended purpose.

## How do you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a system?
Following OOP and Agile principles, I approach designing software like I am painting a portrait. I first identify the core components that are essential to the system's purpose by analyzing the requirements gathered from stakeholders. In the same way you define the head’s shape before painting a nose, these components represent the objects, processes, and relationships that are integral to the system. Developing a solid understanding of the system’s purpose helps ensure that every subsequent design decision contributes to solving the user's problem rather than adding unnecessary complexity.

Once a foundation has been established, I analyze the different types of users who will interact with the system. By understanding each user's goals, responsibilities, and interactions, I can better define how the system must behave and what functionality it must provide. From there, I break the system down into smaller, more intuitive pieces that can be designed, implemented, and tested independently. This modular approach encourages separation of responsibilities, reduces complexity, and makes the system easier to maintain. Additionally, viewing a system as a collection of smaller components allows me to refine each individual piece while still understanding how they contribute to the overall design, similar to how an artist focuses on individual details while maintaining the vision of the entire portrait.

In the future, I hope to continue using techniques such as user stories, use case diagrams, UML diagrams, and iterative prototyping to visualize system relationships and validate design decisions. Like painting, software design is an iterative process where the initial structure provides a foundation, but continuous refinement is necessary to create a final product that accurately represents the intended vision.
